We review the Seat Ibiza Ecomotive from price to economy and all its features

THERE’S no better place to party than the holiday island of Ibiza.

So it seemed the perfect place to celebrate the 25th birthday of the Seat Ibiza, the supermini named after the sunshine island.

Take a Seat girls ... beauties enjoy ride

There have been sales of more than 4million since it was launched, making it the mainstay of the brand and their best-selling model in the UK.

The latest version, launched last year, is as sexy and easy on the eye as the bikini girls who brighten Ibiza’s beaches.

Which is understandable when you realise that the man who designed it is Luc Donckerwolke, also responsible for the Lamborghini Gallardo supercar.

Donckerwolke believes owners of small cars have just as much right to get excited about their wheels as a supercar owner and has made it his mission to deliver sexy Seats.

But the model we were testing had a couple more reasons to celebrate because the Ecomotive also happens to be the most economical and green car Seat have ever built.

How does 76.4mpg on the combined cycle and road tax-exempt C02 emissions of 98g/km grab you? Look at it this way, the money you save on fuel and tax in a year could be a down payment on a holiday in Ibiza.

We decided to put the Ecomotive to the test by driving it around the island to find out just how far you can go on a tank.

more motors

But with a range of 735 miles on a full tank Ibiza was simply not big enough — after two days of driving, the needle barely budged from full.

Which was good news because I worked out that the money I’d saved was more than enough to buy me several extra pints, even at Ibiza prices.

I’d like to say that the Ecomotive was as good to drive as it was to look at but the 1.4litre three-cylinder turbo-diesel is clearly built for economy and not performance.

Changes to the engine management system mean it’s all about maximising fuel economy, a point underlined by 0-62mph in a leisurely 12.7 seconds, but in laid-back Ibiza the lack of power didn’t seem to matter.

There are other fuel-saving measures, too. It’s more aerodynamic than the standard Ibiza and, because the grille is sealed off to improve efficiency, you get lips in front of the wheel arches to further reduce drag.

The air pressure in the tyres has been increased, there’s a sporty but functional roof spoiler and even the profiled door mirrors do their bit to help the aerodynamics. It is also 77lb lighter than the standard car.

And the Ibiza is well equipped with electric front windows, height and reach-adjustable steering wheel, folding rear bench seat, rev counter, three cup holders, air con, six-speaker MP3 compatible radio/CD player, ABS brakes and speed-sensitive steering.

Last but not least, this wallet and eco-friendly supermini doesn’t come with a big premium — the price is a very reasonable £11,995, which is another reason to celebrate.

What to look out for when buying a used Seat Ibiza

While the electrics are generally dependable, the powered windows can take on a mind of their own, either refusing to budge or going up and down of their own accord. Annoying creaks and rattles can also crop up, while lots of wind noise from the front window seals can be an issue – especially on three-door models.

You may feel some weird clunks from the power steering, but these don’t mean anything is about to give up; it just seems to be a characteristic of some Ibizas. Also are stroppy stereos which refuse to talk to whatever MP3 player is plugged in.

A single recall shouldn’t give much cause for concern. It came in June 2012 and affected Ibizas built between April 2010 and November 2011. A faulty catch meant their bonnets could fly open, so be sure to check that that has been seen to.
